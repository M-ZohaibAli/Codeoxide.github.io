Mechanics


Kinematics


Kinematics is the study of motion without considering its causes. It deals with the description of motion in terms of position, velocity, and acceleration. Kinematics is concerned with questions like "how far has an object traveled?", "what is the object's speed at a particular instant?", and "what is the object's acceleration?".

Dynamics


Dynamics is the study of the forces causing motion. It deals with the causes of motion and how forces affect the motion of objects. Dynamics is concerned with questions like "what force is required to move an object?", "what is the net force acting on an object?", and "what is the acceleration of an object due to a given force?".

Newton's Laws of Motion


Newton's Laws of Motion are the three fundamental laws that govern the motion of objects. The first law states that an object at rest will remain at rest, and an object in motion will continue in motion with a constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force. The second law states that the acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on the object, and inversely proportional to its mass. The third law states that for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Conservation Laws


Conservation laws are the principles of energy, momentum, and angular momentum conservation. These laws state that the total amount of energy, momentum, and angular momentum in a system remains constant unless acted upon by an external force.

Work and Energy


Work and energy are closely related concepts in mechanics. Work is the product of force and displacement, and energy is the ability to do work. The relationship between force, work, and energy is governed by the work-energy theorem, which states that the net work done on an object is equal to the change in its kinetic energy.

Circular Motion


Circular motion is the study of motion in a circular path. It is characterized by a constant speed and a changing direction. Centripetal force is the force that keeps an object moving in a circular path.

Gravitation


Gravitation is the study of the force of attraction between objects. The force of gravity is proportional to the product of the masses of the objects and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
< class="text-center">Fluid Mechanics
Fluid mechanics is the study of the behavior of fluids under various conditions. It is concerned with questions like "what is the pressure of a fluid?", "what is the rate of flow of a fluid?", and "what is the viscosity of a fluid?".

Oscillations


Oscillations are periodic motions that repeat themselves over time. Examples of oscillations include a pendulum swinging back and forth and a mass attached to a spring bouncing up and down.

Waves


Waves are disturbances that propagate through a medium. They are characterized by their amplitude, wavelength, and frequency. Examples of waves include sound waves and light waves. The behavior of waves is governed by the principles of wave mechanics.
